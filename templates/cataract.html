What is Cataract ?

A cataract is a clouding of the lens of the eye, which is typically clear. For people who have cataracts, seeing through cloudy lenses is like looking through a frosty or fogged-up window. Clouded vision caused by cataracts can make it more difficult to read, drive a car at night or see the expression on a friend's face. Most cataracts develop slowly and don't disturb eyesight early on. But with time, cataracts will eventually affect vision. At first, stronger lighting and eyeglasses can help deal with cataracts. But if impaired vision affects usual activities, cataract surgery might be needed. Fortunately, cataract surgery is generally a safe, effective procedure.

  • Clouded, blurred or dim vision
  • Trouble seeing at night
  • Sensitivity to light and glare.
  • Fading or yellowing of colors.
  • Double vision in one eyen

    • Reason for Cataract

    Most cataracts develop when aging or injury changes the tissue that makes up the eye's lens. Proteins and fibers in the lens begin to break down. This causes vision to become hazy or cloudy. Some disorders passed down from parents that cause other health problems can increase your risk of cataracts. Cataracts also can be caused by other eye conditions, past eye surgery or medical conditions such as diabetes. Long-term use of steroid medicines also may cause cataracts to develop

    Major types of Cataract

    Nuclear Cataracts

    These cataracts form in the center (nucleus) of the lens. Nuclear cataracts are often associated with aging and typically develop slowly over time. As they progress, they can cause the lens to become yellow or brown, leading to a gradual decrease in visual acuity and an increased difficulty with near vision. Nuclear cataracts can also result in changes in prescription, such as an increase in nearsightedness (myopia).

    Cortical Cataracts

    Cortical cataracts develop in the outer edge (cortex) of the lens. They are characterized by wedge-shaped opacities that start at the periphery of the lens and extend inward over time. Cortical cataracts can cause visual disturbances such as glare, halos around lights, and difficulty with contrast sensitivity. These cataracts can progress relatively rapidly compared to nuclear cataracts.
